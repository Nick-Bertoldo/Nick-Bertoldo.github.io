This study explores the feasibility of utilizing various organic waste materials—rice husk ash, wood ash, corncob granules, and wheat straw—as a 10% fine aggregate replacement in concrete. Mechanical testing revealed that rice husk ash and wood ash provided the most promising results, maintaining acceptable compressive and flexural strengths after 14 days of curing. While wheat straw and corncob granules yielded lower strengths, their potential remains if advanced treatment methods are implemented. A life cycle assessment (LCA) highlighted that while traditional concrete currently exhibits the lowest global warming potential (GWP) per cubic meter when considering mechanical properties, concrete samples incorporating wood ash and rice husk ash demonstrated significant carbon sequestration, resulting in comparable GWP impacts. This suggests their potential as sustainable alternatives, especially in applications with less stringent strength requirements, warranting further research to optimize their performance.
